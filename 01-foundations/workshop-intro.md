# 🎤 Workshop Intro — How I Got Here

I started exploring agents a while ago, and at some point decided to share what I was learning. I should have started earlier. One of those posts — *[How Agents Work: The Patterns Behind the Magic](https://agenticloopsai.substack.com/p/how-agents-work-the-patterns-behind)* — is what caught Yuriy's eye, and that's how we ended up here.

You might ask: *why do we need to understand the internals?*

AI is moving fast, and like any fast-growing technology, it's hard to separate hype from substance. I look at it pragmatically. This technology isn't going anywhere — and as engineers, we need to adapt and learn. Understanding the patterns matters, just like it does for any other technology we use.

I keep coming back to the early web as an analogy.

In the mid-90s, the internet was new and confusing. By the early 2000s, it had changed everything. Not overnight. Not without mistakes. But permanently. Some engineers adapted — they learned HTML, then JavaScript, then frameworks — and realized the web didn't *replace* software engineering, it *became part of it*. Others struggled.

AI feels the same. It won't replace software engineering — it'll become part of it. Engineers who understand how agents actually work will build better systems, debug them more effectively, and design architectures that play to AI's strengths while accounting for its limits.

Just as understanding HTTP, statelessness, and browser rendering made you a better web developer — understanding prompts, tool orchestration, memory, and failure modes will make you a better engineer in an AI-augmented world.

The goal isn't to become an AI specialist. It's to be **fluent enough that when an agentic workflow is the right solution, you recognize it — and when it isn't, you recognize that too.**
